DOSNES

Doubly Stochastic Neighbor Embedding on Spheres

DOSNES is a new method to visualize your data.

It is based on t-SNE with two improvements:

  • Doubly Stochastic Normalization
  • Spherical Embedding

Why Doubly Stochastic Normalization?

t-SNE is the state-of-the-art method for data visualization. However, when the similarity graph in t-SNE has highly non-uniform distribution of node degrees, t-SNE might have an undesirable effect: the nodes of high degree are crowed in the center.

For example, in a co-author graph (NIPS), some authors like Michael Jordan have lots of papers while most people only have a few. As a result, although productive authors such as Hinton and Scholkopf have never co-authored a paper, they are close in the plot, as below.

For another example, in a world trade dataset, some countries like USA and Germany have much more total imports/exports than many others. In the t-SNE visualizations, these countries are crowded in the center, as below

A solution here is to apply doubly stochastic normalization to the similarity graph such that all node have the same degree. Please see our paper below for more details.

Why Spherical Embedding?

  • Doubly stochastic matrices and spheres have a close mathematical relationship. Roughly speaking, a doubly stochastic similarity matrix is likely to induce data embedded on spheres. Please see our paper below for the proofs.
  • A sphere has no center or boundary on its surface. Thus, it eliminates center and boundary effects.
  • Some data are more natural to embed on spheres such as geography data.
  • Data visualization on spherical screens.
